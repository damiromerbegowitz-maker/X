The Lantern and the Sparrow
Nine small scenes

Sparrow: The fence looks taller today.

Lantern Maker: Fences do that when we’re tired. Here—stand on the post; I’ll steady it.

Sparrow: Your lantern is dim.

Lantern Maker: Enough to begin. We can brighten it together.

Sparrow: My wind chime went silent.

Lantern Maker: Not broken—knotted. Joy does this. Hold the string; I’ll unfasten what time tightened.

Sparrow: It sounds small.

Lantern Maker: Small is where music begins.

Sparrow: I am not enough.

Lantern Maker: Enough for what?

Sparrow: For the world.

Lantern Maker: The world is not a mouth to be filled. It’s a place to inhabit. A day asks only to be lived.

Lantern Maker: The forest does not ask the river to be taller.

It welcomes its exact course—the bends, the stones, the hush over deep places.

Measure yourself by the work only you can do: the way you notice, the way you care, the way you return.

Sparrow: How do I walk where I am afraid?

Lantern Maker: Keep a small promise to the next step. Fear travels faster than feet, but it tires sooner.

We go together, and the ground appears.

Lantern Maker: Fear is the shadow of the path. It stretches where the light stands behind you.

We don’t fight the shadow. We align the light—step, breath, value—and walk.

Sparrow: I flew ahead, only to see if I could.

Lantern Maker: You didn’t need certainty. You needed a beginning.

Borrow light when you must. It’s yours while you carry it.

For Daria
May you remember: the sky does not measure the worth of a wing—it simply makes room. Walk gently where you doubt, and let small lights add up.
